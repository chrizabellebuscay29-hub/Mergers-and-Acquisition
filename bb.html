  • The analysis of eleven publicly listed Philippine companies shows that Inventory Turnover Ratio (ITR) exhibited fluctuations across the pre- and post-merger periods, but no statistically significant improvement was observed after mergers and acquisitions.

  • Overall, the empirical results reveal that mergers and acquisitions did not produce a statistically significant strategic or financial impact on the selected performance indicators during the post-liberalization period.

This quick snapshot highlights study design and approach â€” use the visual cards above to orient before reading detailed summaries below.
  • The study examines the strategic impact of mergers and acquisitions (M&A) on publicly listed Philippine companies by comparing pre-merger and post-merger financial performance.
  • It evaluates M&A impact using four key indicators: Inventory Turnover Ratio (ITR), Profit After Tax (PAT), Book Value per Share (BVPS), and Return on Net Worth (RoNW) to assess operational efficiency, profitability, and shareholder value.
  • The research tests the null hypothesis that there is no significant difference in firmsâ€™ financial performance during the five years before and five years after the M&A event.
  • An ex post facto research design is employed, utilizing secondary data from audited financial statements and annual reports of eleven (11) publicly listed companies on the Philippine Stock Exchange.
  • Data covering a 10-year period (5 pre-M&A and 5 post-M&A) were analyzed using SPSS, applying descriptive statistics, paired sample t-tests, and trend analysis, with findings interpreted alongside relevant M&A literature.

CConclusion

âš 
M&A outcomes vary â€” the transaction alone isn't a guarantee of improved financial performance; integration and firm-specific factors matter.
  • Mergers and acquisitions among publicly listed Philippine companies did not result in statistically significant improvements in inventory efficiency, profitability, or shareholder value.
  • The absence of significant differences implies that the expected synergies from M&A activities may take longer to materialize or may be influenced by external factors.
  • Financial performance indicators alone may be insufficient to fully capture the strategic impact of mergers and acquisitions.
  • The business environment provided opportunities for corporate restructuring through mergers and acquisitions; however, the outcomes varied across firms, indicating that M&A effectiveness depends on firm-specific conditions and execution.
  • Strategic success following mergers depends not only on the transaction itself but also on post-merger integration and management effectiveness.
